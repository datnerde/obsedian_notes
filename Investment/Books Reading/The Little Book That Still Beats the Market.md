### Key Components of the Magic Formula:

1. **Return on Capital (ROC)**:
    - This metric measures the profitability of a company relative to the capital it employs. It’s calculated as: $$ ROC = \frac{\text{Earnings Before Interest and Taxes (EBIT)}}{\text{Net Working Capital + Net Fixed Assets}}$$​
    - Companies with a high ROC are efficient at generating profits from their capital, indicating a strong business.
2. **Earnings Yield (EY)**:
    - This metric evaluates how cheap a company is relative to its earnings. It’s calculated as: $$EY = \frac{\text{EBIT}}{\text{Enterprise Value (EV)}}$$
    - A high earnings yield suggests the company is undervalued.

### How the Magic Formula Works:
1. **Rank Companies**:
    - Greenblatt’s formula ranks companies based on their ROC and EY. Each company gets two ranks: one for ROC and one for EY. The ranks are then combined.
2. **Select Top Stocks**:
    - The companies with the best combined ranking (lowest sum of ROC rank and EY rank) are considered the most attractive investments.
3. **Diversification**:
    - Greenblatt suggests selecting 20-30 top-ranked companies and holding them for a year. This diversification helps manage risk.
4. **Rebalance Annually**:
    
    - After a year, the portfolio is rebalanced by selling off stocks that no longer meet the criteria and buying new top-ranked stocks.

### The Philosophy Behind the Magic Formula:

- The Magic Formula is based on the belief that high-quality companies (those with a high ROC) that are available at a good price (those with a high EY) will outperform the market over time. It systematically combines value investing (buying undervalued stocks) with a focus on quality (profitable companies).

### Performance and Limitations:

- Greenblatt’s backtesting and real-world results have shown the Magic Formula can outperform the broader market over time. However, like any strategy, it may underperform in certain periods, and it requires discipline and patience.

### Conclusion:

The Magic Formula offers a straightforward, rules-based approach to investing that combines two powerful principles: buying good companies at bargain prices. It’s designed for long-term investors who are willing to stick with the strategy through various market conditions.
