# Stock Screener
In _The Intelligent Investor_, Benjamin Graham provides specific criteria, or "screeners," to help investors select stocks. These screeners are primarily aimed at identifying companies that are financially sound, undervalued, and likely to provide steady returns. Graham offers slightly different criteria for **defensive** and **enterprising** investors.

## 1. **Screener for Defensive Investors**:
![[Pasted image 20240820131437.png]]
Defensive investors are those who prefer a more conservative approach, focusing on minimizing risk and preserving capital. Graham recommends the following criteria for selecting stocks:

1. **Adequate Size of the Enterprise**:
    - **Industrial companies**: At least $100 million in annual sales (this threshold can be adjusted for inflation).
    - **Utility companies**: A certain minimum in assets (adjusted for modern standards).
2. **Strong Financial Condition**:
    - **Current ratio** (current assets divided by current liabilities) of at least 2:1.
    - **Long-term debt** should not exceed the company’s net current assets.
3. **Earnings Stability**:
    - Positive earnings for each of the past 10 years.
4. **Dividend Record**:
    - Uninterrupted dividend payments for at least 20 years.
5. **Earnings Growth**:
    - A minimum increase of at least one-third in per-share earnings over the past 10 years (using a three-year average at the beginning and end).
6. **Moderate Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E)**:
    - Current P/E ratio should not exceed 15 times the average earnings of the past three years.
7. **Moderate Price-to-Book Ratio (P/B)**:
    - Price should be no more than 1.5 times the book value. Alternatively, the product of the P/E ratio and the P/B ratio should not exceed 22.5.
## 2. **Screener for Enterprising Investors**:
Enterprising investors are willing to take on more risk for potentially higher returns. Graham suggests the following criteria:

1. **Adequate Financial Condition**:
    - **Current ratio** of at least 1.5:1.
    - Debt should not exceed 110% of net current assets.
2. **Earnings Stability**:
    - No deficits in the last five years.
3. **Dividend Record**:
    - Some history of dividend payments, though not as stringent as for defensive investors.
4. **Earnings Growth**:
    - A positive growth in earnings over the past five years.
5. **Moderate Price-to-Earnings Ratio**:
    - Current P/E should not exceed 12 times the average earnings of the past three years.
6. **Moderate Price-to-Book Ratio**:
    - Price should not be more than 1.2 times the book value.
7. **Select Undervalued Stocks**:
    - Look for stocks trading at a significant discount to their intrinsic value, which could be based on low P/E ratios, low price-to-book ratios, or other valuation metrics.
## 3. **Special Situations and Bargains**:

Graham also discusses opportunities for enterprising investors who are willing to look for “special situations” or bargains. These might include:
- Companies in distress but with strong potential for recovery.
- Stocks trading below their net current asset value (known as "net-nets").
- Stocks with temporary problems but solid underlying businesses.

## **Conclusion**:

Graham’s stock screening criteria are designed to filter out companies that are financially weak or overpriced, ensuring that investors are left with a list of robust, undervalued companies with a margin of safety. The focus on conservative metrics like earnings stability, dividend history, and reasonable valuations helps mitigate risk, which is the cornerstone of Graham’s value investing philosophy.

# Margin of Safety

The **margin of safety** is a central concept in Benjamin Graham's investment philosophy, introduced in _The Intelligent Investor_. It refers to the difference between a stock's intrinsic value (the true worth of a company based on its fundamentals) and its market price.

### Key Aspects of Margin of Safety:

1. **Definition**:
    - The margin of safety is the buffer or cushion that protects investors from errors in judgment, unforeseen market declines, or unexpected negative events. It is the amount by which the stock's market price is below its intrinsic value.
2. **Purpose**:
    - The margin of safety reduces the risk of loss by ensuring that even if the intrinsic value has been miscalculated or the market faces adverse conditions, the investment is still likely to be profitable.
3. **Application**:
    - Suppose an investor calculates that the intrinsic value of a company is $100 per share, but the stock is currently trading at $70. The margin of safety here is 30%, meaning the investor has a buffer in case their valuation was too optimistic or the market turns against them.
4. **Graham’s Approach**:
    - Graham suggested that a margin of safety should be substantial, typically buying stocks when they are 30% to 50% below their intrinsic value. This conservative approach reflects his focus on minimizing risk and protecting capital.
5. **Importance in Value Investing**:
    - The margin of safety is vital for value investors because it acknowledges the inherent uncertainty and risk in investing. Even with careful analysis, there’s always the possibility of being wrong, so a margin of safety helps safeguard against this.
6. **Dynamic Nature**:
    - The margin of safety is not a fixed number; it can vary based on market conditions, the investor’s confidence in their analysis, and the stability of the company’s earnings. Higher risk investments may require a larger margin of safety.
